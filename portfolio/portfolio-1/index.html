    Single-neuron mechanisms of error self-monitoring

    Recommended citation: Z. Fu, D. A. J. Wu, I. Ross, J. M. Chung, A. N. Mamelak, R. Adolphs, U. Rutishauser, Single-neuron correlates of error monitoring and post-error adjustments in human medial frontal cortex. Neuron. 101, 165-177.e5 (2019), doi:10.1016/j.neuron.2018.11.016

    Background

    To err is human. In our daily life, we often make errors that deviates from our goals, caused by interference or distraction. These sources of distraction can be external (e.g.,a sudden loud bang), or internal (e.g., intrusive thoughts, or automatic habitual responses that are goal incompatible). The amazing fact is that we can often monitor our own errors. For example, we decide to pick up some grocery on our way home from work; just as we approach the traffic junction where we need to make a right turn to the grocer, we keep going straight habitually because that is the way home, only to realize moments later that we made the mistake. What are the neural mechanisms that allow us to monitor our own errors? The error-related negativity (ERN) is a event-related potential (ERP) recorded from scalp electroencephalogram (EEG) that reliably signal the occurrence of errors. ERN has been a major workhorse for studying error processing in humans. It has shown promise as an endophenotype that index cognitive control dysfunction in psychiatric diseases. Using inverse modelling, the sources of ERN has been attribucted to the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC). However, the inverse problem of finding ERP sources is ill-defined; there are inifinitely many solutions. Due to this, some studies attributed the source of ERN to pre-supplmentary motor area (pre-SMA). Moreover, the link between ERN and neuronal spiking activity has not been demonstrated. Given the importance of error monitoring in daily life and its implication in psychiatric disease, it is important to advance the field by studying error processing at the single neuron level directly in humans, and relating ERN to neuronal signaling. We study error processing by simultaneously recording single neuron activity and intracranial EEG using depth electrodes implanted epilepsy patients. These patients have drug-intractable epilepsy that requires intracranial EEG monitoring to localize the serizure onset zone.

    Summary

    We recorded in the medial frontal cortex of patients performing a Stroop task and found neurons that track self-monitored errors and error history in dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC) and pre-supplementary motor area (pre- SMA).

    We simultaneously recorded intracranial EEG from the most medial macro contact (see the top figure for a diagram of electrode). We recorded robustly intracranial ERN from macro contacts implanted within the dorsal ACC and pre-SMA. The ERN also first appeared in pre-SMA and followed by dACC after ~50ms, mirroring the latency difference in error neuron firing. The ERN amplitude and latency in the two areas were strongly correlated, suggesting a hierarchical organization where pre-SMA first detects error and pass along the information for further processing in dACC.

    (A) ERN from an example session (colors; red for error, blue for correct), aligned at button press (t = 0, thick vertical gray bar). Inset, distribution of iERN latencies for the same data. Thin vertical bar marks 100 ms after button press. (B) Comparison of iERN latency across all sessions. The iERN peak occurred significantly earlier in the pre-SMA compared to the dACC. (C) Trial-by-trial correlation of iERN latency (upper) and iERN amplitude (lower) between pairs of iERNs recorded simultaneously in dACC and pre-SMA. For both, the correlation coefficients have a mean significantly greater than zero. Red vertical bars show the mean values.

    Error neuron responses were correlated with iERN amplitude on individual trials. In dACC, such error neuron-iERN synchrony and responses of error-history neurons predicted the magnitude of PES. These data reveal a human single-neuron correlate of the ERN and suggest that dACC synthesizes error information to recruit behavioral control through coordinated neural activity.

    (A) ERN amplitude correlated significantly with the spike rates of error neurons (type I). The likelihood ratio peaked around $400 ms after button press. t = 0 is button press. Gray shading delineates the extent of the significant cluster as determined by a cluster-based permutation test. Note that the sig- nificant cluster started earlier in pre-SMA. (B) The correlation between iERN amplitude and error neuron spike rates (as a function of time; quantified as the likelihood ratio in model comparison) predicted the extent of post-error slowing (PES) in the dACC. t = 0 is button press. Gray shading delineates the extent of the significant cluster as determined by a cluster-based permutation test (p < 0.05). The same analysis in the pre-SMA did not yield a statistically significant relationship. (C) The spike rates of error-integrating neurons in dACC around the time of stimulus onset predicted PES.
