    The geometry of domain-general performance monitoring

    Recommended citation: Z. Fu, D. Beam, J. M. Chung, C. M. Reed, A. N. Mamelak, R. Adolphs, U. Rutishauser, The geometry of domain-general performance monitoring in the human medial frontal cortex. Science. 376, eabm9922 (2022), doi:10.1126/science.abm9922

    Background and Significance

    We constantly monitor how well we perform the task at hand, often without relying on any external feedback. For example, we monitor whether have made a mistake, whether we have experienced any conflict/difficulty and whether we have got distracted. Such monitoring triggers cognitive control to compensate for the performance disturbances and ensure we achieve our goals, effectively forming a “feedback control loop”. A key feature of this control loop is its flexibility: we can monitor our own performance across many different tasks and optimize our control of behaviors in accordance with the fluctuating environmental demands. Dysfunctional self-mentoring and cognitive control result in a loss of behavioral flexibility and may underlie many psychiatric disorders. Given the scientific and clinical interest, these topics have generated a large literature in the past two decades, but important open questions remain unresolved: what are the neural mechanisms that enable performance monitoring and cognitive control to adapt to ever-changing environmental demands, and to function across many different cognitive domains/novel tasks?

    Summary

    At Cedars-Sinai, we have the unique opportunity to monitor the activity of single neurons from human patients implanted with electrodes as part of their clinical care. We recorded from 1431 single neurons in the medial frontal cortex (MFC) across 34 patients. Subjects pressed, as fast as possible, one of three buttons to indicate the ink color of color words (Task 1, Stroop task, e.g., “red” printed in green, distraction by the word meaning) or to report the unique number (Task 2, Multi-source Interference Task, e.g., “2 1 1”, distraction by flankers and/or target location).

    The Multi-Source Interference Task (left) and the color-word Stroop task (right). In both tasks, subjects were asked to indicate his or her response as quickly as possible by a button press, and the response was always followed by an accuracy feedback (screen blanked for 1s).

    Using a hierarchical Bayesian model, we showed that subjects estimated the probability of encountering with a conflict trial and adjusted how fast and accurate they respond accordingly.

    The conflict probability estimation process (left) and the decision process modelled as a drift diffusion (right). Shown is the MSIT model, which has the six variables learning rate (&alpha), Simon probability (qsi), Flanker probability (qfl), observed Simon conflict (osi), observed Flanker conflict (ofl), and RT. Observables (trial congruency, RT, and outcome) are shown in gray, model parameters are in white. Arrows indicate information flow.

    Single MFC neurons encoded conflict, conflict probability (“CP”) and errors in one or both tasks, intermixed at similar anatomical locations within the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC) and pre-supplementary motor area (pre-SMA).

    A. Recording locations B-D. Example neurons

    These complex and high-dimensional patterns of activity, however, gave rise to a simple population-level representation whose geometry allowed readout of all three variables trial-by-trial with the same accuracy in both tasks, without sacrificing the accurate coding of performance monitoring information specific to each task.

    A. Domain-general docoding of error. B. Domain-general decoding of conflict (Stroop and Simon) in two tasks. C. The domain-general coding dimension for conflict still supports separation between MSIT conflict conditions. D. The domain-general coding dimension for CP still supports separation of CP levels within a single task.

    Representation of CP was consistent with the dynamics of a line attractor, with levels of CP stably maintained throughout the trial. The neural states indicating the presence of multiple types of conflict were equal to the vector sum of the states indicating each individual type of conflict, suggesting a compositional code. These results suggest a novel neural mechanism for performance monitoring across cognitive domains.
